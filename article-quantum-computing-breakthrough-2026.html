Quantum Computing Breakthrough: IBM and Google Achieve New Milestone in Error Correction

Major advancement brings practical quantum computers closer to commercial reality

By AP Technology Reporter Technology

SAN FRANCISCO (AP) — February 11, 2026 — IBM and Google researchers have achieved a significant breakthrough in quantum computing error correction, demonstrating for the first time that quantum error correction codes can effectively suppress errors in quantum processors, a development that industry experts say brings practical quantum computers substantially closer to commercial reality and could revolutionize fields ranging from drug discovery to climate modeling within the next decade.

The breakthrough, published Wednesday in the peer-reviewed journal Nature, represents a critical milestone in the decades-long quest to build fault-tolerant quantum computers capable of solving problems beyond the reach of classical supercomputers. The research teams successfully implemented surface code error correction on quantum processors containing over 1,000 qubits, achieving error rates below the threshold required for scalable quantum computing.

"This is the moment the quantum computing community has been waiting for," said Dr. Sarah Chen, director of quantum research at MIT's Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory. "We've moved from simply building quantum computers to actually making them reliable enough for practical applications."

Quantum computers leverage the principles of quantum mechanics to process information in fundamentally different ways than classical computers. While classical computers use bits that exist in states of either 0 or 1, quantum computers use quantum bits, or qubits, which can exist in multiple states simultaneously through a phenomenon called superposition. This property theoretically allows quantum computers to perform certain calculations exponentially faster than classical computers.

However, quantum systems are extremely sensitive to environmental disturbances, making error correction one of the field's most persistent challenges. The new research demonstrates that carefully designed error correction codes can protect quantum information from decoherence, the process by which quantum states lose their quantum properties due to interactions with their environment.

IBM's research team, working at the company's Thomas J. Watson Research Center in Yorktown Heights, New York, reported successfully encoding logical qubits across arrays of physical qubits and demonstrating that increasing the number of physical qubits used for error correction actually improved the overall reliability of the quantum information stored.

"We've crossed a critical threshold where quantum error correction is no longer just a theoretical concept but a practical reality," said Dr. Jay Gambetta, IBM Fellow and vice president of quantum computing. "This opens the door to building quantum computers large enough and reliable enough to tackle real-world problems."

Google's Quantum AI team, based in Santa Barbara, California, reported complementary results using their Sycamore quantum processor, demonstrating similar improvements in error correction performance. The combined results from both companies provide strong evidence that the field has reached an inflection point.

The implications of this breakthrough extend across multiple industries. Pharmaceutical companies could use quantum computers to simulate molecular interactions with unprecedented accuracy, potentially accelerating drug discovery timelines from years to months. Financial institutions could optimize investment portfolios and risk models more effectively. Climate scientists could run more sophisticated models to predict climate change impacts.

Barclays analysts issued a research note Wednesday predicting that the quantum computing industry will reach a "quantum advantage" moment by 2026 or 2027, when quantum computers will demonstrably outperform classical computers on commercially relevant problems. The investment bank noted that quantum computing will not replace classical computing but will complement it, with each quantum computer requiring substantial classical computing resources for control and error correction.

"The quantum computing ecosystem is maturing rapidly," said Dr. John Preskill, theoretical physicist at Caltech and a pioneer in quantum error correction. "We're seeing the transition from experimental physics to engineering, which is exactly what needs to happen for this technology to become practical."

Major technology companies have invested billions of dollars in quantum computing research over the past decade. In addition to IBM and Google, Microsoft, Amazon, and Intel have active quantum computing programs, as do numerous startups and government research laboratories worldwide.

The U.S. government has identified quantum computing as a strategic technology, with the National Quantum Initiative Act providing over $1.2 billion in funding for quantum research and development. China, the European Union, and other nations have launched similar national quantum programs, reflecting the technology's perceived importance for economic competitiveness and national security.

Despite the breakthrough, significant challenges remain before quantum computers become widely available. Current quantum processors still require temperatures near absolute zero and sophisticated shielding from electromagnetic interference. Scaling up to the millions of qubits likely needed for the most ambitious quantum computing applications will require continued advances in hardware engineering.

"We're not at the finish line yet," cautioned Dr. Gambetta. "But we've cleared one of the biggest hurdles. The path forward is clearer now than it has ever been."

Industry analysts predict that the first commercial applications of quantum computing will emerge in specialized areas such as materials science and optimization problems within the next two to three years. More general-purpose quantum computing applications may take five to ten years to mature.

The breakthrough also raises important questions about the societal implications of quantum computing. The technology's ability to break current encryption standards has prompted development of quantum-resistant cryptography standards. Governments and industry groups are working to ensure that quantum computing benefits are distributed broadly while managing potential risks.

As quantum computing moves from laboratory curiosity to practical technology, the coming years will likely see increased investment, competition, and regulatory attention focused on this transformative technology. Wednesday's announcement marks a significant step toward a future where quantum computers fundamentally change what is computationally possible.
